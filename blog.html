    2024 Presidential Election Predictions

    It is perhaps hasty to make predictions this early on in the election season, so my ideas here are likely to be proven wrong in time. As such, I plan to continue updating my thoughts on this post as we properly enter the season, with election news, polling, and personal research informing all changes. Before we look at the predictions, there are a few key considerations to take into account regarding my predictions:

    1. The colors and shades of colors on the maps below represent either a Democrat majority (Blue) or a Republican majority (Red) and a darker color represents a more firm partisanship for each respective party. The darkest shade (solid) represents a state with a margin >10% between the two candidates. The second darkest shade (likely) represents a state with a margin >5%. The third darkest shade (lean) represents a state with a margin >2%. The fourth darkest shade (tilt) represents a state with a margin >0%.

    2. There are two possible scenarios in any proper U.S. Presidential Election: Either one candidate receives the majority of all electoral votes (270 votes or more) Or, in the case of a tie or when no candidate receives the majority of the votes (e.x. both candidates receive exactly 269 votes) the President is then decided by the House of Representatives. This is quite unlikely in the current state of U.S. politics, but not impossible.

    3. We don’t yet know who the candidates are. Okay, so this is a big problem for these predictions. As of writing this article, it is probable that Joe Biden, the incumbent president will be the nominee for the Democrats. As for the GOP, the most likely candidates are former president Donald Trump and Florida Governor Ron DeSantis. While these things may change, I believe that Donald Trump will be the eventual Republican nominee so I’ll be basing my predictions on this decision.

    As you can see, states are abbreviated by their shorthand names (e.x. New Hampshire becomes NH) and each number represents the amount of electoral votes each state has. 270 votes is the minimum required for a candidate to win an outright majority.     5. Again, these predictions are subject to change and are NOT final or necessarily accurate.

    Alright, on with it! Let’s see what I believe will be the result of the 2024 U.S. Presidential Election as of June 18, 2023:

    The Prediction: The Probable Picture

    Would you believe it? The Democrats hold onto the Presidency by a rather close margin. This outcome is quite similar to 2020, with several changes and notable trends worth discussing.

    1. Nevada? What happened? Nevada has been trending more Republican for over a decade. We’ve seen narrow Presidential victories for Democrats in 2016 and 2020, a close-fought U.S. Senate race in 2022, and even a convincing Republican victory in the 2022 gubernatorial election. While the race will be razor-thin, I predict Nevada will return to its pre-2008 tradition and vote for the Republican presidential candidate.

    2. Georgia, Colorado, and Arizona become safer for Democrats. Changing demographic trends and voting patterns in the South and Sunbelt regions have induced key victories for Democrats in recent elections, notably in 2020 where Georgia and Arizona flipped to Democrats in the Presidential and U.S. Senatorial elections. Colorado has been voting for Democratic candidates by greater margins in every election but one since 2000. While I believe the nationwide political winds in 2024 will blow in favor of the GOP, these three states will anchor for Democrats nonetheless.

    3. Wisconsin Flips. In 2020, Wisconsin was determined by the third smallest margin of any state (after Georgia and Arizona) with a difference of just 0.63% between Dems and the GOP. Four years earlier, the state voted for Trump by a difference of nearly a percentage point. Sparse polling indicates that the race is near-even in a potential matchup between Biden and Trump. I predict that overall, Midwest and Rust Belt states in general will experience outsized pushback against the incumbent President compared to the nation as a whole.

    4. Republicans grow stronger in Florida, Michigan, Pennsylvania, Minnesota, Virginia, New Mexico, and Ohio. Factors contributing to a general tendency toward Trump over Biden nationally include high inflation, scandals (As of January 12, the Biden document scandal is beginning to unfold), and low incumbent approval ratings all may well contribute to a stronger showing for Donald Trump in 2024.
